The 11 Best Ice Cream Scoops of 2024

I scream, you scream, and as Blake Lively said in an Instagram post while visiting the Museum of Ice Cream: "Now I get why everyone screams." As one of the most universally loved desserts by children and adults alike, ice cream is the perfect summertime treat. However, the ice cream truck might not come ‘round your neighborhood too often, so you may need to master the fine art of scooping your own treat.

Rather than struggling with an ice cream scoop that won’t dig, is tricky or messy to use, or feels uncomfortable in your hands, consider one of our top selections. "The type of scoop absolutely matters and can make a huge difference between a tweaked wrist and a beautiful scoop," Tyler Malek, co-founder and head ice cream maker at Salt & Straw tells PEOPLE.

While Malek says a spoon or spatula can get the job done, if you invest in an ice cream scoop, you should steer clear of trigger scoops (better suited for cookie dough), spades, and scoops with pointed tips. "And pay no mind to scoops with anti-freeze inside. Unless you’re scooping all day, there’s no point," he adds.

We did the ‘hard’ work of testing 24 ice cream scoops for durability, efficiency, effectiveness, design, and user experience — and enjoyed the fruits of our labor. After multiple tests totaling 10 hours of scooping, we found our winners.

Kick off summer with the very best ice cream scoops that PEOPLE Tested.

Things to Consider Before Buying an Ice Cream Scoop

Handle Length and Material

As the person scooping out the ice cream, the scoop's handle will have a huge affect on your overall ice cream serving experience. In our testing, we found that the material of the handle directly affected our comfort level. For example, a stainless steel handle is durable, but not one you'd like to use for a long period of time. On the opposite end of the spectrum, a handle with a rubberized grip, like the OXO Good Grips Ice Cream Scoop, makes for a more comfortable scooping experience.

The length of the handle also makes a big difference. If you're scooping ice cream out of a large container or looking to get to the bottom of your pint easily, then a longer handle like the one on the Kitchen Aid Gourmet Ice Cream Scoop would be a good option for you.

Scooper Shape and Trigger Style

The ice cream scooper's shape directly affects the ice cream balls' formation. A traditional ice cream scooper, like the Farberware Ice Cream Scoop, will create larger balls of ice cream via an inward-spiral formation, and then you'll release the scoop into a bowl with another spoon, your finger, or a gentle tap.

An ice cream scooper with a trigger style like the Good Cook Ice Cream Scoop, will usually create smaller, more perfectly rounded ice cream balls. The trigger style allows you to drop the balls right into your ice cream bowl or dessert plate for a contactless experience.

In our testing, we also found that spades are not ideal for the everyday ice cream eater; they are targeted toward professionals. For the everyday consumer, it's more difficult to get into small pints with a spade and it's equally as difficult to dig into the ice cream and shape it into a ball. These types of scoopers are best left to the ice cream experts.

How We Tested Ice Cream Scoops

To get to the bottom of the best ice cream scoops — and the bottom of containers — we put 24 scoops through two tests over the course of 10 hours. First, we powered our way through a pint container of Häagen-Dazs to assess the scoop’s performance, scooping out ice cream with a firmer texture and maneuvering in a pint container. We used each scoop to take three scoops, using our left hand at least once. 

We then noticed how well the scoop fit into the pint container and how easy it was to maneuver. Then, we considered the grip: was it comfortable to hold? Did it quickly release the ice cream and provide neat scoops for plating? Lastly, we washed and dried the scoop and ran it through the dishwasher if applicable. 

For our second test, we dug into a half-gallon container of Blue Bell ice cream, known for its airier, lighter texture. This time, we removed five scoops and concentrated on the surface without digging too deep. 

These ice cream scoops ranged in price from $9.58 to $43.25 at the time of testing. Some ice cream scoops were PR samples, and others were purchased at full price, but testers had no knowledge of either while performing the tests. They took the retail price into account at the very end of the test when assessing the overall value of each ice cream scoop.

After these tests, we noted each ice cream scoop’s design, special features, grip, overall performance, and durability. After rating all of the scoops, we named our top 11 and they are currently being tested at home.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What are professional ice cream scoops made of?

    You may notice various scoops or spades when you visit your local ice cream shop. Generally speaking, though, the most traditional ice cream scoops have a rubber or plastic handle for easy gripping. And the body of the scoop is likely made out of stainless steel or aluminum, so it can dig into frozen desserts effortlessly.

  • What is the standard ice cream scoop size?

    Do you want one, two, or three scoops for your little treat? And how do you know how much you’re consuming? On average, a scoop of ice cream is about 4 ounces, though, of course, this will vary by ice cream shop and type of scoop.

  • What should I look for in an ice cream scoop?

    Like any kitchen gadget, what matters in an ice cream scoop depends on the individual. But from our experience, we found that the length and material of the handle are essential. If the shaft is too long, it's unwieldy while scooping, and if it is too short, then you’ll end up with ice cream all over your hands. We also discovered that having handles made with silicone or other gripping materials allowed us to add force/pressure to scoop into more compact ice creams. 

    The scooper shape affects how the ice cream balls are formed and how easily those balls release into the bowl. And while it might seem like a trigger-style scoop would work best, we found those are better for cookie dough and batter. We found that the gap between the moving scraper created uneven ice cream balls and was more challenging to clean. In our opinion, these types of tools were better off for other jobs.

    Lastly, while spades are great for commercial ice cream shops, they are not as convenient for home cooks. While they cut into the ice cream easily, you would need to shape them into ice cream balls manually. These larger scoops also don’t fit easily into smaller containers, like pints.
